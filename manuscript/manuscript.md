# Abstract
Generalized pollination systems, wherein a single plant species attracts diverse flower visitors, are widespread in nature. The common perennial weed _Achyranthes bidentata_ (Amaranthaceae) is visited by diverse insects; however, detailed reports on its flower visitors are not available. Our pilot observation confirmed that _A. bidentata_ was frequently visited by diverse insects including Hymenoptera, Lepidoptera, and Diptera. We also observed the visiting patterns of the two major visitors, lycaenid butterflies and honeybees. This in-depth observation revealed that lycaenid butterflies made long-distance visits between individual plants, whereas honeybees were inclined to visit multiple flowers within an individual plant. These findings pave the way to study pollinator-mediated plant-plant interactions at intraspecific and interspecific levels, such as geitonogamy and reproductive interference.

Keywords: _Achyranthes_, Generalist, Pollination   
Category: Notes and Insights  

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

As sessile organisms, many plant species rely on mobile animals to transfer pollen. Although some plant species have co-evolved with a particular pollinator, many other species remain as generalists to be visited by diverse pollinators [@willmer2011pollination]. In a generalized pollination system, pollination effectiveness and/or efficiency has long been investigated among multiple flower visitors [@herrera_daily_1990; @waser_generalization_1996; @neeman_framework_2010]. It is well recognized that bees can carry a large amount of pollen and thereby serve as an effective pollinator [e.g., @heinrich_foraging_1976; @waser_comparison_1982; @kandori_diverse_2002-1]. However, quantitative surveys have shown that abundant pollinators may not always be the best contributor to plant reproduction. For example, frequent visitors often revisit flowers within a single individual, resulting in substantial self-pollination called geitonogamy [@de_jong_geitonogamy_1993; @karron_influence_1995; @hessing_geitonogamous_1988]. Genetic paternity analyses have shown that less frequent visitors, such as flower beetles and butterflies, can largely contribute to outcrossing [@broyles_effective_1991; @matsuki_pollination_2008-1]. At the interspecific level, flower visitors may play a critical role in interspecific pollen transfer and competitive exclusion [@levin_competition_1970; @waser_interspecific_1978; @takakura_effective_2011; @katsuhara_prior_2019]. Thus, the quantitative observation of multiple visitors provides a basis for an inclusive understanding of plant reproductive ecology.    

The genus _Achyranthes_ comprises over 30 species distributed worldwide (POWO 2026). This genus is native to Asia and Africa but was also introduced to Middle-to-South America and Tropical-to-Subtropical Old World (POWO 2026). Some _Achyranthes_ species are known as medicinal and ornamental plants called 'chaff flowers', while others produce inconspicuous petalless flowers. Rather than floral ecology, seed dispersal of _Achyranthes_ has attracted more ecological attention, as _Achyranthes_ seeds easily attach to animal hairs [e.g., @bullock_comparative_1977; @de_lange_achyranthes_2004]. A few studies have examined the population genetic structure and breeding systems of widely distributed species, such as Asian _A. bidentata_ and _A. aspera_ [@shivanna_reproductive_2014; @yan_assessment_2016; @yang_landscape_2017]. These genetic structures and reproductive success are thought to depend on the pollination system of _Achyranthes_. Although _A. bidentata_ is known to be visited by wild bees [@putra_foraging_2009], in-depth observations of multiple flower visitors are still lacking.  

In this study, we found that _A. bidentata_ individuals were recurrently visited by diverse insects. We also observed the visiting patterns of two major visitors, lycaenid butterflies and honeybees. Based on these observations, we briefly discuss how these insects can mediate plant-plant interactions at intraspecific and interspecific levels.  


# 2 | MATERIALS AND METHODS

## 2.1 | Study plants
We investigated _Achyranthes bidentata_ Blume and its flower visitors (Fig. 1). The native distribution range of this plant species is from South to East Asia (POWO 2026). In Japan, _A. bidentata_ often occurs in sunny wastelands and blooms from late summer to autumn (Fig. 1a and b). Seeds easily adhere to animal fur or human clothes. Although there are several synonyms for _A. bidentata_, our nomenclature follows the Ylist of Japanese plants (Yonekura and Kajita 2003-). According to the Ylist, _A. bidentata_ has four subspecies, some of which might be misidentified as _A. aspera_. Specifically, what we referred to as _Achyranthes bidentata_ in this study was _A. bidentata_ Blume var. _fauriei_ (H. Lév. et Vaniot) so-called “Hinata-inokozuchi” in Japanese.  

## 2.2 | Study sites and pilot observation
Our field survey was conducted at the Yoshida campus of Kyoto University, Kyoto, Japan (35°01′54″N, 135°47′14″E). The study area was located near an open playground. The density of individual _A. bidentata_ was so high that a 1 m^2^ patch included spikes from >10 individuals (average = 12.7, SD = 2.1, number of measured patches = 10). In addition to _A. bidentata_, the bushkiller _Cayratia japonica_ and long-bristled smartweed _Persicaria longiseta_ bloomed during the study period. Given the active conditions for flower visitors, we made all observations during the daytime (9:00-15:00) on sunny days.  

To ascertain whether _A. bidentata_ produced entomophilous flowers, we first conducted pilot observations in the morning and afternoon in Kyoto. This observation was performed twice (morning and afternoon) on 8 September 2010. We counted insect visits on the spike of _A. bidentata_ per ten minutes up to the order level. The sex of the individual insects was not distinguished. The area of the observed patch was approximately 4 m^2^. Dominant visitors were captured and identified as much as possible.  

To confirm whether _A. bidentata_ attracted multiple flower visitors, we conducted additional observations at another site. We used a rural area in Fujiwara-cho, Mie, Japan (35°09′5″N, 136°30′06″E). This study site was located in a meadow near an abandoned field, including the water willow _Justicia procumbens_ and creeping woodsorrel _Oxalis corniculata_ as the other blooming plants. Our observations were performed twice (morning and afternoon) on 10 October 2010. We counted insect visits following the same method described above for the Kyoto site.  

## 2.3 | Visiting patterns of major insects
To identify potential agents of plant-plant interactions, we focused on the visiting patterns of abundant insects. In addition to visiting frequency, we examined the following three aspects: (a) repeated visits within an individual plant, (b) visitation time, and (c) movement distance. This line of field surveys was conducted at the Kyoto site where lycaenid butterflies (_Zizeeria maha_ ssp. _argia_, _Lycaena phlaeas_ ssp. _daimio_, and _Everes argiades_ ssp. _hellotia_) and honeybees (_Apis cerana_ ssp. _japonica_) were abundant. We confirmed the potential of these major visitors to carry _A. bidentata_ pollen by observing pollen grains adhering to plant anthers and insect bodies under electron microscopy (Fig. 1c-e).  
  

_(a) Intra-individual visitation_: We first compared intra-individual visitation rate and frequency between lycaenid butterflies and honeybees per 30 min. In this observation, we defined a single visit as movement between _A. bidentata_ flowers. In addition to the number of visits, we recorded whether the movement was inter- or intra-individual. The 30-min observation was performed five times on 20 September 2010 afternoon, 26 September 2010 morning and afternoon, 5 October 2010 afternoon and 12 October 2010 afternoon. The observed area was approximately 4 m^2^. A leaving movement out of the 4-m^2^ area was also counted as a single visit as long as it was made between _A. bidentata_ flowers.

_(b) Visitation time_: We then compared the time spent on _A. bidentata_ spikes between lycaenid butterflies and honeybees. We recorded the time from landing to departure by 0.01 second order using a stopwatch. This observation was performed twice on 7 October 2010 afternoon and 12 October 2010 afternoon. We examined 40 individuals per survey, yielding a total sample size of 80 honeybees and butterflies.  

_(c) Movement distance_: We finally compared the distance of inter-flower movement between lycaenid butterflies and honeybees. We measured the distance between the _A. bidentata_ spikes from 10 to 100 cm. A movement longer than 1 m was recorded as >100 cm. This observation was performed three times on 26 September 2010 afternoon, 29 September 2010 afternoon, and 12 October 2010 afternoon. For each observation, the same number of individuals was measured for butterflies and honeybees. The entire dataset consisted of 20 individuals on 26 September 2010; 30 individuals on 29 September 2010; and 51 individuals on 12 October 2010, yielding a total sample size of 101 honeybees and butterflies.

## 2.4 | Statistical analysis
We used generalized linear mixed models (GLMMs) to test the effects of the two visitor types (honeybee or butterfly) on the total number of visits, proportion of intra-individual visits, visitation time, and movement distance. Regarding each response variable, we assumed Poisson error with a log link function for the total number of visits, binomial error with a logit function for the proportion of intra-individual visits, Gamma error with a log link function for the visitation time, and ordered multinomial error with a logit link function for the movement distance. The fixed effects were the two visitor types. The difference in survey dates was considered a random effect. Statistical significance was determined using a likelihood ratio test with or without each term, with the significance level being set at  = 0.05.  

All statistical analyses and visualizations were performed using R version 4.3.0 [@Rcite]. The default setting was applied to the link functions of the GLMs and GLMMs. Regarding add-on R packages, we utilized the lme4 [@citeRlme4] and ordinal [@citeRordinal] packages for GLMMs; and the tidyverse package [@citeRtidyverse] for data handling and visualization. All source codes and original data generated in this study are available at GitHub (<https://github.com/yassato/Achyranthes>) and Zenodo (<https://doi.org/xxxx>) repositories.  

# 3 | RESULTS

## 3.1 | Diverse insect visitors to _A. bidentata_ flowers
We observed 11 species of flower visitors to _A. bidentata_ at the two sites (Table 1). In Kyoto, the most and second-most abundant orders were Hymenoptera and Lepidoptera, respectively (Fig. 2). Specifically, we observed the native honeybee _Apis cerana_ ssp. _japonica_ and three lycaenid butterflies, _Zizeeria maha_ ssp. _argia_, _Lycaena phlaeas_ ssp. _daimio_, and _Everes argiades_ ssp. _hellotia_, as major visitors (Table 1). In contrast, the most abundant order in Mie was Diptera (Fig. 2). Hymenoptera was observed as the second most abundant order in Mie (Fig. 2), but its abundant species was not a honeybee but a scoliid wasp, _Campsomeris prismatica_ (Table 1). These observations confirm that _A. bidentata_ can attract diverse insects as a generalist flowering plant.  

## 3.2 | Visiting patterns of major insects
To gain quantitative insights, we then examined the visiting patterns of two major insects, lycaenid butterflies and honeybees, in Kyoto. We found that honeybees visited more frequently than lycaenid butterflies (Poisson GLMM, $\chi^2_1$ = 691.95, $p$ < 0.001), but these honeybees were more likely to have intra-individual visits (binomial GLMM, $\chi^2_1$ = 103.17, $p$ < 0.001: Fig. 3a). We further observed visitation time and flight distance to compare movement patterns between butterflies and honeybees. Lycaenid butterflies stayed longer on flowers than honeybees, as shown by their significant difference in visitation time (Gamma GLMM, $\chi^2_1$ = 176.77, $p$ < 0.001: Fig. 3b). We also found that lycaenid butterflies were more likely to move between distant flowers of _A. bidentata_ than honeybees (ordered multinomial GLMM, $\chi^2_1$ = 91.86, $p$ < 0.001: Fig. 3c and d).  

# 4 | DISCUSSION

## 4.1 | Insight
In a generalized pollination system, previous studies suggested that the best pollen carrier might not always be the most efficient pollinator due to geitonogamy [@karron_influence_1995; @broyles_effective_1991; @matsuki_pollination_2008-1]. Regarding major flower visitors, we found that honeybees frequently visited _A. bidentata_, but this resulted in recurrent visitations to multiple flowers within an individual (Fig. 3a). This repeated visitation could hinder outcrossing in _A. bidentata_. In contrast, lycaenid butterflies might be able to transfer pollen to distant individuals, although they did not frequently visit _A. bidentata_. Besides intraspecific interactions, interspecific pollen transfer would be a clue to understand reproductive interference between related species in insect-pollinated plants [@takakura_effective_2011; @katsuhara_prior_2019]. The genus _Achyranthes_ includes many species with various subspecies worldwide (POWO 2026), In Japan, _A. bidentata_ has a subspecies named _A. bidentata_ var. _japonica_ (Yonekura and Kajita 2003-), which inhabits a shady forest edge adjacent to a sunny habitat of _A. bidentata_ var. _fauriei_. These two subspecies were previously classified into different species as _A. japonica_ and _A. fauriei_, given their ecological and morphological differences (Okuyama 1936). In this respect, lycaenid butterflies may be able to transfer pollen between these (sub)species. Taken together, our study identified potential agents of interspecific interactions as well as intraspecific interactions involving _Achyranthes_, thereby opening an avenue to investigate pollinator-mediated plant-plant interactions for future studies. 

## 4.2 | Limitation
We still lack critical information on plant breeding systems to understand the fitness consequences in _A. bidentata_. For instance, we know little about self-compatibility in _A. bidentata_ reproduction, despite its importance in understanding the impact of geitonogamy on fruit-set. In addition, it remains unknown why petalless _A. bidentata_ flowers can attract such a diverse range of flower visitors. Although we found pollen grains adhered to lycaenid butterflies and honeybees (Fig. 1d and e), other resources for pollinators have yet to be examined. The flowers of _A. bidentata_ may be able to produce not only pollen but also nectar as they let lycaenid butterflies stay (Fig. 3b). To explore adaptive significance, we ultimately need to quantify both male and female fitness in _A. bidentata_. Other than plant breeding systems, our data lack information on the individual sex of lycaenid butterflies. Male visitors are more likely to visit flowers by chance than females [@kishi_nested_2022], which might differentiate the flight distance between male and female butterflies. These limitations should be considered, but our data provide the first step to understand the reproductive ecology of _Achyranthes_ species.
